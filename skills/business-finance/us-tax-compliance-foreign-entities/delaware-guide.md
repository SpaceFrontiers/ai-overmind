# Delaware Entity Formation & Compliance Guide for Foreign Owners

Delaware is the most popular state for incorporation in the United States, with over 1.5 million entities registered. It's especially favored by venture capital-backed startups and public companies.

## Why Choose Delaware?

### Key Advantages

1. **Business Court of Chancery**
   - Specialized business court established 1792
   - Judges (not juries) with deep corporate law expertise
   - 200+ years of case law precedent
   - Fast, predictable dispute resolution
   - Globally recognized legal framework

2. **Flexible Corporate Law**
   - Most sophisticated and modern corporation law
   - Delaware General Corporation Law (DGCL)
   - Regularly updated to reflect business needs
   - Allows maximum flexibility in structuring
   - Protects director/officer from personal liability

3. **Venture Capital & Investor Preference**
   - **95%+** of VC-backed startups incorporate in Delaware
   - Investors familiar with Delaware law
   - Standard term sheets assume Delaware entity
   - Easier fundraising and eventual exit/IPO

4. **No State Income Tax on Out-of-State Income**
   - Delaware corporations with no Delaware operations pay no state income tax
   - No sales tax
   - Low personal property tax

5. **Privacy for Shareholders**
   - Shareholder names not required in public filings (corporations)
   - Directors/officers are public, but not shareholders
   - LLCs require no member disclosure

6. **Corporate Governance Flexibility**
   - Can issue multiple classes of stock
   - Can have staggered boards
   - Can implement anti-takeover provisions
   - Allows blank check preferred stock

### Key Disadvantages

1. **Higher Costs**
   - Franchise tax can be substantial ($450-$200,000+ for corporations)
   - Annual report fee: $50
   - Higher registered agent fees
   - Total annual costs typically higher than Wyoming/Nevada

2. **Franchise Tax Calculation Complexity**
   - Two calculation methods; must pay higher amount
   - Based on authorized shares OR assumed par value capital
   - Can be very expensive for companies with large capitalizations
   - LLCs pay flat $300/year (simpler)

3. **Dual Filing if Operating Elsewhere**
   - If you have physical presence in another state, must register there too
   - Becomes a "foreign entity" in operating state
   - Additional fees and compliance in both states

4. **Less Privacy for Corporations**
   - Directors and officers must be disclosed (unlike Wyoming LLCs)
   - Annual report is public
   - More transparent than some other states

## Formation Process

### Step 1: Choose Entity Type

**Corporation (Most Popular in Delaware)**:
- **C-Corporation**: Most common; required for VC funding
- **S-Corporation**: Not available if any owner is non-resident alien
- Best for: Startups, tech companies, businesses seeking investment

**LLC (Alternative)**:
- Simpler structure, lower annual fees ($300 vs $450+)
- Less investor familiarity
- Good for: Real estate, consulting, holding companies
- Not preferred by VCs

### Step 2: Name Reservation (Recommended)

- Reserve online at corp.delaware.gov
- Cost: $75
- Valid for 120 days
- Search availability first (free): icis.corp.delaware.gov

### Step 3: File Certificate of Incorporation

**For Corporation**:

**Filing Method**:
- Online: corp.delaware.gov (fastest)
- By mail: Delaware Division of Corporations
- By fax: (302) 739-3812

**Filing Fees**:
- Standard (24-hour): $89 + $15 filing + $50 county filing = **$154 minimum**
- Same day: $154 + $100 = $254
- 2-hour rush: $154 + $500 = $654
- 1-hour rush: $154 + $1,000 = $1,154

**Required Information**:
- Corporate name (must include Corp., Inc., Company, Association, Club, Foundation, Fund, Institute, Society, Union, Syndicate, Limited, or abbreviation)
- Registered agent name and Delaware street address
- Number of authorized shares (default: 1,500 shares)
- Par value (or no par value)
- Incorporator name and address (can be anyone)

**Stock Authorization**:
- Default: 1,500 shares = $89 filing fee
- If > 10,000 shares: Additional fees apply
- Strategic: Authorize only what you need initially (can increase later)

**For LLC**:

**Filing Fee**: $90
**Required Information**:
- LLC name (must include "Limited Liability Company" or "LLC")
- Registered agent and office address
- Organizer name and address

### Step 4: Obtain EIN from IRS

- Apply at irs.gov/ein
- Free
- Required for all entities
- Foreign owners: May need to apply by fax if online system doesn't work
- Receive EIN immediately (online) or in 4-6 weeks (fax)

### Step 5: Draft Corporate Documents

**Certificate of Incorporation** (File with Delaware):
- Minimum required information
- Keep simple; details go in bylaws
- Typically 1-3 pages
- Public document

**Bylaws** (Keep Private):
- Board structure and meeting procedures
- Officer roles and appointments
- Shareholder meeting rules
- Stock transfer restrictions
- Indemnification provisions
- Typically 10-30 pages
- **Not filed** with Delaware

**For Corporations, Also Prepare**:
- Stock certificates
- Stock ledger
- Initial board resolutions
- Organizational minutes
- Subscription agreements (if selling stock)

**For LLCs**:
- Operating Agreement (private, not filed)

### Step 6: File Beneficial Ownership Report (BOI)

**Federal Requirement** (Corporate Transparency Act):
- File with FinCEN (not Delaware)
- Deadline:
  - Formed in 2024: 90 days
  - Formed on/after Jan 1, 2025: **30 days**
- Report at: boiefiling.fincen.gov
- Information: Beneficial owners (25%+ ownership), ID documents, addresses

### Step 7: Appoint Registered Agent

**Requirements**:
- Must have physical Delaware street address
- Available during business hours
- Receives legal documents and official notices

**Popular Delaware Registered Agents**:
- Harvard Business Services: $50/year
- Incorp: $50/year
- CT Corporation: $300+/year (enterprise)
- Corporation Service Company (CSC): $300+/year
- Northwest Registered Agent: $125/year

## Annual Compliance

### 1. Delaware Franchise Tax (Corporations)

**Due Date**: March 1 annually

**Two Calculation Methods** (pay whichever is higher):

**Method 1: Authorized Shares Method**
- 5,000 shares or fewer: **$175**
- 10,000 shares or fewer: **$250**
- Each additional 10,000 shares: +$85
- Maximum: **$200,000**

Example:
- 1,500 shares (typical startup): $175
- 10,000,000 shares: $85,000+

**Method 2: Assumed Par Value Capital Method**
- Gross assets (from federal return) ÷ Total shares issued = Assumed par value per share
- Assumed par value × Authorized shares = Assumed par value capital
- Tax based on assumed par value capital (complex tables)
- Minimum: $400
- Can be much lower than authorized shares method if you have few assets and many authorized shares

**Strategy**:
- Calculate both methods; pay lower amount
- Use Delaware franchise tax calculator: corp.delaware.gov/paytaxes
- Consider limiting authorized shares initially
- Can amend to increase shares when raising capital

**Late Penalty**: $200 + 1.5% interest per month

### 2. Annual Report

**Due Date**: March 1 (same as franchise tax)
**Fee**: $50
**File Online**: corp.delaware.gov

**Required Information**:
- Registered agent details
- Directors and officers names and addresses
- Stock information

**Late Penalty**: $125 + potential dissolution

### 3. Delaware LLC Annual Tax

**Amount**: Flat **$300** (much simpler than corporations)
**Due Date**: June 1
**No Report Required**: Just pay the tax
**Late Penalty**: $200

**Why LLCs are Simpler**:
- No franchise tax calculation
- No annual report
- Just one flat payment
- Lower total cost for most entities

### 4. Federal Tax Filings

**All Foreign-Owned Entities**:
- Form 5472 (if 25%+ foreign-owned): April 15
- C-Corp: Form 1120
- LLC: Form 1065 (partnership) or 1040-NR (disregarded)
- BOI updates: 30 days after changes

## Delaware Franchise Tax Strategies

### For Corporations: Minimize Franchise Tax

**Strategy 1: Limit Authorized Shares Initially**
- Authorize only what you need (e.g., 10 million shares)
- Amend to increase when raising capital
- Amendment cost: $150-$200
- Can save thousands in franchise tax

**Strategy 2: Use Assumed Par Value Method**
- If your assets are low relative to authorized shares
- Calculate both methods annually
- Switch methods year-to-year as needed

**Strategy 3: Consider LLC Instead**
- If not seeking VC funding
- Flat $300/year vs. $400+ for corporation
- Trade-off: Less familiar to investors

**Example Comparison**:

**Scenario**: Foreign-owned entity, no VC plans
- **Delaware LLC**: $300/year + $50 agent = $350/year
- **Delaware C-Corp** (10M shares, low assets): $400-$1,000/year + $50 agent
- **Wyoming LLC**: $60/year + $125 agent = $185/year

### For Corporations: When High Franchise Tax is Worth It

**You should pay Delaware's higher costs if**:
- Planning to raise venture capital (required by VCs)
- Building high-growth tech company
- Planning eventual IPO or M&A exit
- Want Chancery Court protections
- Need sophisticated corporate governance (multiple share classes, etc.)

**You can skip Delaware if**:
- Running lifestyle business or consultancy
- Not seeking institutional investment
- Want to minimize costs
- Privacy is priority (consider Wyoming LLC)
- Holding company only

## Delaware vs Wyoming: Decision Matrix

| Factor | Choose Delaware | Choose Wyoming |
|--------|----------------|----------------|

## Corporate Governance Best Practices

### Required Actions

**Annual**:
- Hold annual shareholder meeting (can be virtual)
- Hold annual board meeting
- Document meetings in minutes
- File Delaware annual report
- Pay franchise tax

**As Needed**:
- Board approval for major decisions
- Shareholder approval for fundamental changes
- Update stock ledger for any transfers
- Amend certificate if structure changes

### Maintaining Corporate Formalities

**Critical for Foreign Owners**:
- Keep corporate and personal finances separate
- Maintain detailed records
- Follow bylaws for all actions
- Document all board/shareholder decisions
- Hold meetings (even if informal/virtual)

**Why It Matters**:
- Preserves limited liability protection
- Protects against "piercing corporate veil"
- Important for tax treatment
- Required by investors/auditors

## Stock Structuring for Startups

### Common Delaware Structure

**Authorized Stock** (in Certificate):
- 10,000,000 shares common stock
- 5,000,000 shares preferred stock (blank check)

**Initially Issue** (Keep Most Unissued):
- Founders: 8,000,000 common shares (80% of common)
- Employee pool: 2,000,000 common shares reserved (20%)
- Preferred: None issued until first funding round

**Why This Structure**:
- Preserves shares for future fundraising
- Allows ESOP without diluting founders immediately
- Standard structure VCs expect
- Franchise tax based on authorized, not issued (unfortunately)

### Founder Vesting

**Standard Terms**:
- 4-year vesting
- 1-year cliff (no vesting until year 1)
- Monthly vesting thereafter
- Acceleration on exit (single or double trigger)

**Implementation**:
- Restricted Stock Purchase Agreement
- 83(b) election (critical within 30 days!)
- Vesting schedules in stock ledger

## Common Pitfalls for Foreign Owners

### 1. Missing March 1 Franchise Tax Deadline

**Problem**: $200 penalty + 1.5% monthly interest; eventual dissolution
**Solution**: Set calendar reminder for February; pay early
**Cost**: Can be $400-$200,000 depending on structure

### 2. Not Filing 83(b) Election

**Problem**: If founders receive vested stock, IRS taxes as compensation at vesting
**Solution**: File 83(b) within 30 days of stock issuance
**Impact**: Can save tens of thousands in taxes

### 3. Authorizing Too Many Shares

**Problem**: Franchise tax based on authorized shares
**Example**: 100M shares authorized = $8,500+ annual tax (even if only 100 shares issued)
**Solution**: Start with 10-20M shares; amend later when raising capital

### 4. Choosing Delaware When Not Needed

**Problem**: Paying $400+ annually when Wyoming would cost $185
**Reality**: Only need Delaware if seeking VC or planning IPO
**Solution**: Honestly assess funding plans; can reincorporate later if needed

### 5. Forgetting to Calculate Both Franchise Tax Methods

**Problem**: Overpaying by using wrong calculation method
**Solution**: Use Delaware's online calculator; try both methods
**Savings**: Can be thousands per year

### 6. Not Maintaining Corporate Formalities

**Problem**: Mixing personal/business funds; no board minutes
**Result**: Potential loss of liability protection
**Solution**: Treat corporation formally even if you're sole shareholder

### 7. Missing BOI Reporting

**Problem**: $500/day penalty (up to $10,000)
**Solution**: File with FinCEN within 30 days of formation

## Delaware Entities + Foreign Tax Treaties

### Corporate Residency for Tax Treaties

**Issue**: Tax treaties define "residence" for treaty benefits

**Delaware Corporation**:
- US tax resident (incorporated in US)
- Qualifies for US tax treaties
- Can claim benefits on income from treaty countries
- Subject to Limitation on Benefits (LOB) provisions

**Requirements to Claim Treaty Benefits**:
1. Delaware corporation is US resident (automatically qualifies)
2. Beneficial owners qualify under treaty LOB
3. Corporation has sufficient substance (not just shell company)
4. File Form W-8BEN-E to claim treaty benefits

**Potential Issue for Foreign-Owned Delaware Corp**:
- Some treaties deny benefits if ultimate ownership is non-treaty country
- Need to review specific treaty LOB provisions
- May need "active trade or business" to qualify

## Reincorporation & Conversion

### Wyoming → Delaware (Common Path)

**When to Do It**:
- Raising first VC round (VCs require Delaware)
- Preparing for IPO
- Getting serious investor interest

**Process**:
1. Form new Delaware corporation
2. Merge Wyoming entity into Delaware entity
3. Exchange stock/membership interests
4. File dissolution in Wyoming
5. Transfer assets and contracts

**Cost**: $2,000-$5,000 in legal fees
**Time**: 2-4 weeks

**Tax Treatment**: Generally tax-free if done properly

### Delaware → Wyoming (Less Common)

**When to Consider**:
- Decided not to pursue VC funding
- Want to reduce ongoing costs
- No longer need Delaware structure

**Caution**: 
- Investors may object
- Can signal "scaling back" ambitions
- Less common path

## Conclusion

Delaware is the **gold standard** for:
- **VC-backed startups**: Required by 95%+ of VCs
- **Tech companies**: SaaS, software, platforms
- **Companies planning IPO**: NYSE/NASDAQ prefer Delaware
- **Complex structures**: Multiple share classes, anti-takeover provisions
- **M&A targets**: Buyers prefer Delaware for predictability

**Choose Delaware if**:
- You plan to raise institutional capital (VC/PE)
- You're building a high-growth tech company
- You want sophisticated corporate governance
- You need Court of Chancery protections
- Your investors/advisors recommend it
- You can afford $400-$1,000+/year in fees

**Skip Delaware if**:
- You're running a lifestyle business
- No plans for institutional funding
- Cost is primary concern
- Privacy is critical priority
- Holding company or real estate LLC
→ **Consider Wyoming instead** ($185/year, stronger privacy, asset protection)

**Bottom Line**: Delaware's higher costs are worth it **if and only if** you're on a VC/IPO track. For other foreign-owned businesses, Wyoming usually makes more sense.
